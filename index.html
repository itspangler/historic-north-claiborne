The Story of North Claiborne Avenue, New Orleans, LA

If you were standing at the intersection of Ursulines and North Claiborne in 1947, the intersection at which the above photo was taken, you'd be struck by a few things. Clocking in at 191-feet across, you'd notice that the sheer width of the street is breathtaking. The massive neutral ground – a local term for what folks in the US generally call a "median" – would be lined by a row of live oak trees, verdant and generously shaded. Children might be playing in this de facto public park; pedestrians would ... ... ...

In New Orleans, North Claiborne Avenue has long been a bustling hub of Black business and local African American culture. This remains true today, as Claiborne is a popular gathering spot during second line parades. However, the space itself is significantly different. In 1966, construction began on the I-10 expressway, which demolished the live oaks and the neutral ground that had been central to the character of the Claiborne Avenue business district. ... ... ...

This project is an attempt to pivot from narratives of destruction and dispossession in Black communities throughout the 20th century. Instead, it highlights the life on Claiborne Avenue in the 1940s and '50s. Use the Sanborn maps below to explore selected businesses within the Claiborne corridor. Hover your mouse over the polygons for more information, or simply examine the maps.
