TO EXPLORE THE NORTH CLAIBORNE HISTORIC DISTRICT IN NEW ORLEANS, LA

New Orleans' "Black Wall Street":
The North Claiborne Commercial Corridor

IF YOU WERE STANDING at the intersection of Ursulines and North Claiborne in 1947, the intersection at which the above photo was taken, you'd be struck by a few things. Clocking in at 191-feet across, you'd notice that the sheer width of the street is breathtaking. The massive neutral ground – a local term for what folks in the US generally call a "median" – would be lined by a row of live oak trees, verdant and generously shaded. Children might be playing in this de facto public park; pedestrians would ... ... ...

In New Orleans, North Claiborne Avenue has long been a bustling hub of Black business and local African American culture. This remains true today, as Claiborne is a popular gathering spot during second line parades. However, the space itself is significantly different. In 1966, construction began on the I-10 expressway, which demolished the live oaks and the neutral ground that had been central to the character of the Claiborne Avenue business district, erecting instead the snaking leviathan of concrete that remains to this day.

This project is an attempt to pivot from narratives of destruction and dispossession in Black communities throughout the 20th century. Instead, it highlights the life on Claiborne Avenue in the 1940s and '50s.

Sanborn Maps as Research Tools

Sanborn maps have a rich history as research tools. Developed in the mid-19th century to assess risk for fire insurance, they have proved an invaluable tool for historical research, thanks to their meticulous detail and accuracy. The Library of Congress holds over 25,000 Sanborn maps in more than 3,000 US Cities, all available for perusal at their website. Instructions for Navigating the Maps

The four Sanborn maps below have been lightly annotated to highlight select Black businesses and institutions that were active prior to the construction of I-10. Some are still active today, and although the items highlighted were not always eradicated due to the construction of the overpass, the annotations are meant to gesture toward the critical community operations that the North Claiborne corridor served, including education, insurance, and pharmaceutical needs to the Black community.

  • Double-click or use the button in the top-left corners of the maps to zoom in and out (scrolling is disabled).
  • Click a polygon to learn more information about the item it contains.
  • Click the 'X,' or anywhere else on the map, to close a popup after opening.
  • See the key for understanding Sanborn map abbreviations.
